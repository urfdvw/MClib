Experiments on the measure¶

chi-square like measure¶

$$\begin{array}{l} D_{\chi^2 } =\int \frac{\pi^{\prime } {\left(x\right)}^2 }{\pi \left(x\right)}\;\mathit{dx}-1\\ =\int \frac{\sum_m {\left\lbrack w^{\left(m\right)} \delta \left(x-x^{\left(m\right)} \right)\right\rbrack }^2 }{\pi \left(x\right)}\;\mathit{dx}-1 \end{array}$$

because $$\frac{\sum_m {\left\lbrack w^{\left(m\right)} \delta \left(x-x^{\left(m\right)} \right)\right\rbrack }^2 }{\pi \left(x\right)}=\left\lbrace \begin{array}{cc} 0 & x\not= x^{\left(m\right)} \\ \frac{{w^{\left(m\right)} }^2 {\delta \left(0\right)}^2 }{\pi \left(x^{\left(m\right)} \right)} & x=x^{\left(m\right)} \end{array}\right.$$ Therefore $$D_{\chi^2 } =\sum_m \frac{{w^{\left(m\right)} }^2 {\delta \left(0\right)}^2 }{\pi \left(x^{\left(m\right)} \right)}-1$$ TO remove the infinity $$\tilde{D} =\frac{D_{\chi^2 } +1}{{\delta \left(0\right)}^2 }=\sum_m \frac{{w^{\left(m\right)} }^2 }{\pi \left(x^{\left(m\right)} \right)}$$

normalized distance¶

With one modification, the measure is much less sensitive to the size of the samples.

$$\tilde{D}_N =M\sum_m \frac{{w^{\left(m\right)} }^2 }{\pi \left(x^{\left(m\right)} \right)}$$

Sample from different distributions and measure the distance¶

The y-axis is always log measure, and x-axis is always $\tau$.

$$ x\sim N\left(\left\lbrack \begin{array}{c} \tau \\ 0\\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{array}\right\rbrack ,\mathit{\mathbf{I}}\right) $$
In [7]:
tau = np.linspace(-20.0,20.0,500)
note.plot(note.sampleshift, sq.Dchi2N, pi, tau)

The true position, $\tau=0$, has the smallest distance

$$ \begin{array}{l} c \sim \mathrm{binomial}\left(\tau \right)\\ \mathrm{if}\;c==1\\ \;\;\;\;x_{i\;} \overset{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim} \;\mathrm{uniform}\left(-3,3\right)\\ \mathrm{else}\\ \;\;\;\;x\sim \pi \left(x\right) \end{array} $$
In [8]:
tau = np.linspace(0.0,1.0,500)
note.plot(note.sampleshape, sq.Dchi2N, pi, tau)

$\tau=0$ gives the true target distribution, where the distance is the smallest.

$$ x \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\ \tau \cdot \mathbf{I}) $$
In [9]:
tau = np.linspace(0.0001,5.0,500)
note.plot(note.samplescale, sq.Dchi2N, pi, tau)

True scaling, $\tau = 1$ does not have the smallest distance. The distance is minimized when all samples are at the peak of the target distribution, i.e. $x\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{0})$.
